Simone de Beauvoir

Feminist Existentialism

Born in France, in 1908, she was a thinker, novelist, and one of the most important representative of the atheist existentialist movement at her time. Also was an excelse figure in the defense of women's rights, and a fighter for the right of the legal abort.

In her bibliografy it can be found a lot of titles, one of the most popular was a novel called 'Les Mandarins', written on 1954 and winner of the Goncourt Award.

Graduated on philosophy, till 1943 devoted herself to teaching at the lyceums of Marseille, Rouen and Paris. Along with Jean-Paul Sartre, Albert Camus and Maurice Merleau-Ponty, among others, of the magazine 'Tiempos Modernos'.

This was a very important political and cultural reference of half French thought of the twentieth century, and it's first issue went out on October 15, 1945. At 1949 she published the book 'Le Deuxième Sexe', this represented a theoric starting point for different feminist groups and became a classic work of contemporary thought.

Simon de Beauvoir was not only an icon of the demands and movements of women who fight for gender equality, but also for the eradication of social practices that reduce women to the background. She wrote novels, essays, biographies and monographs on political, social and philosophical issues. However, the fundamental theme of Simone's intellectual, literary and social activity was the woman.

She thought that in order to rescue the woman it was necessary to give her back her freedom. In 1943 'L'Invitée' was published; In this novel, the writer described, through fictional characters, the relationship between Sartre, Olga and herself, while elaborating a philosophical reflection on the struggle between consciences and the possibilities of reciprocity.


"The oppressor would not be so strong if he did not have accomplices among the oppressed themselves."
